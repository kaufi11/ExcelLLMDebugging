Summary of workbook structure and contents

Workbook purpose
- A research/reference workbook collecting national/regional energy and greenhouse‑gas emission factors and time series from multiple sources (GHG Protocol, NRCan, EIA, IPCC, EU, etc.). Sheets provide source links, raw time series, emission factors (various units), and conversion formulas to g CO2/kWh.

Sheets (brief)
1. Overview
   - Index of data sources with URLs and short descriptions (GHG Protocol, NRCan, AIE, EIA, eGRID, IPCC EFDB, EEA, etc.).
   - Sample tables from sources: CO2 g/kWh by country/Annex groups (years 1990–1999), example country rows (Canada, United States).
   - Notes on where sector spreadsheets live and sample download links.

2. NRCAn data
   - Sectored (Commercial/Institutional, Residential) three‑panel layout: (left) energy use by year and type (PJ), (center) emissions by year and type (megatonnes), (right) computed emission factors (g/kWh).
   - Key formulas: EF for “all fuels” and for “electricity” use use unit conversions:
     - Jx (All fuels EF) = (Fyear * 10^12 * 3.6*10^6) / (Byear * 10^15)
     - Kx (Electricity EF) = (Gyear * 10^12 * 3.6*10^6) / (Cyear * 10^15)
     (these convert megatonnes→grams and PJ→kWh to get g/kWh)
   - Residential + Commercial combined EF computed as simple average: J36..J47 = (Residential EF + Commercial EF) / 2.

3. Aus
   - Australian fuel energy and emission factors: electricity by state (MJ/kWh and g CO2/kWh), statewide fuel CO2 factors (kg/GJ) and fuel properties (GJ/tonne).
   - Key value: Australia average electricity EF listed ~1,051 g/kWh; state range shown (e.g., Victoria 1,467 g/kWh; Tasmania listed as 2 g/kWh — appears to be a data anomaly).

4. EIA_US factors
   - State/region CO2, CH4, N2O emission factors in various units: lbs/kWh, short tons/MWh, metric tons/MWh and calculated grams/kWh.
   - Conversion formulas used repeatedly:
     - gms/kWh = lbs/kWh * 454 (grams per pound)
     - CH4/N2O gms/kWh from lbs/MWh: gms/kWh = (lbs/MWh * 454) / 1000
   - Contains state‑by‑state values (e.g., New England CO2 0.98 lbs/kWh → E5 formula =B5*454 → ~445.9 g/kWh).
   - Note: a few rows show inconsistent/typo formula variants (e.g., some G formulas use *454/10^3 vs *454*10^3), check those cells.

5. EU Data
   - Time series (1990–2000) of total greenhouse gas emissions (CO2e, million tonnes) by country and EU aggregates (EU15).
   - Clean country × year table suitable for trend analysis (e.g., Germany ~1,219 Mt CO2e in 1990 → ~988 Mt in 2000).

6. Energy Overview USA
   - Long time series (1949–2001) EIA energy table (Billion Btu) with production, imports, exports, consumption, fuel splits (fossil, nuclear, renewable).
   - Several placeholder/revised markers [R], P (prelim.) and notes.

7. US GHG emissions
   - Summary tables of U.S. GHG emissions 1990–2001 (Million metric tons) by gas and by carbon‑equivalent (MMT C equivalent).
   - Key totals: CO2 ~5,003 Mt (1990) rising to ~5,855 Mt (2000); total (C‑equivalent) ~1,683 Mt C (1990) → ~1,907 Mt C (2000).

Key units and conversions used across the workbook
- Energy: PJ (petajoules), MJ/kWh, Billion Btu, GJ/tonne
- Emissions: megatonnes, million tonnes CO2e, million metric tons
- Emission factors: g CO2/kWh, kg/GJ, lbs/kWh, tons/MWh
- Common conversion constants:
  - 1 lb ≈ 454 g
  - 1 MWh = 1,000 kWh
  - PJ → kWh conversion used in NRCAn formulas via factors 10^12, 3.6*10^6, 10^15 (to align units)

Notable values / flags
- GHG Protocol sample: U.S. sample electricity CO2 ~596 g/kWh; Canada sample ~193–215 g/kWh across 1990–1999.
- Australia average electricity EF unusually high (~1,051 g/kWh); individual state extremes (Victoria 1,467 g/kWh). Tasmania row shows 2 g/kWh — likely erroneous.
- Some cells in Energy Overview USA show [R] (revised) or [P] (preliminary).
- A few formula inconsistencies/typos in EIA_US sheet (verify G‑column formulas around row 20 and similar).

Overall layout & navigation
- Overview sheet acts as a catalog of sources and contains example excerpts.
- Data sheets are organized as time‑series tables and sector panels with embedded conversion formulas to produce comparable EFs (primarily g CO2/kWh).
- Useful for cross‑checking emission factors across countries/regions and for calculating sectoral EFs from energy and emissions data.
