Findings — main error
- Cell E19 on sheet "Q1" contains the literal value 6 instead of the discounted cash‑flow formula used in the rest of that row. All other D19, F19, G19 are formulas but E19 is a hard number.

Why this is wrong (short)
- Row 19 is one discount-rate scenario. E19 should be the present value of the t=1 cash flow at that row's discount rate. The hard-coded 6 makes the row sum (B19) and the plotted NPV curve incorrect at that rate and can shift apparent IRR roots.

Fix (exact and simple)
- Replace E19 with the correct formula (copy the same structure used in the other discounted cells). Example to enter in E19:
  =$E$2/(1+$J19)^$E$1
- Or copy E4 (or any proper discounted-cell) and paste down into row 19 so the cell becomes the correct formula referencing J19.

Quick verification steps
1. After fixing, check B19 (SUM of D19:G19) — it will change.  
2. Turn on Show Formulas (Ctrl+`) or use Trace Precedents to find any other accidentally overwritten formula cells.  
3. Recalculate and re-check the IRR (E48) and any NPV curve—they will now reflect the correct discounted value.

Optional cleaner alternative
- Instead of per-cell discount formulas, you can compute NPV per rate with:
  =NPV(rate, range_of_future_CFs) + time0_cashflow
  e.g. =NPV($J19,$E$2:$G$2)+$D$2
  This reduces the chance of a single-cell overwrite breaking the curve.
