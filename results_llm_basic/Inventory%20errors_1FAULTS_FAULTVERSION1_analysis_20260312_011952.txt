Short answer
- The spreadsheets have prior-year ending inventory errors that change COGS and net income, but those prior-period errors were NOT closed to retained earnings. That missing offset is why Assets ≠ Liabilities + Equity.
- Fix: adjust retained earnings (opening equity) for the amount of the prior-year inventory error, or correctly restate the prior-year ending inventory. Then the balance sheet will balance.

What’s wrong (specific)
- 2001 ending inventory and 2002 ending inventory were misstated (see Sheet2: B11 = 179800+17000 and D11 = 147200-25000).
- In Sheet1 retained earnings for later years are simply =current year net income (B33 = C15, D33 = E15, F33 = G15). They do not include corrections for the prior-year inventory errors, so equity is missing the balancing entry.
- Result: Total Assets change (inventory), Net Income changes, but Total Liabilities + Equity doesn’t change by the same amount → imbalance.

How to fix it (simple steps and exact cell changes)
Option A — Correct prior-period inventory (restate prior year)
- Put the correct ending inventory in the prior year (e.g. change B11 and D11 to their corrected amounts). That flows through beginning inventory, COGS, net income and retained earnings and will restore balance.

Option B — If you keep the misstated prior-year balances, add the correction to retained earnings (this is what Sheet3 shows). For the sheet you gave, change the retained earnings formulas for the later years:
- Change D33 from =E15 to =E15 + 17000 (adds the 2001 inventory understatement to 2002 opening equity).
- Change F33 from =G15 to =G15 - 25000 (removes the 2002 inventory overstatement from 2003 opening equity).
After these changes Total liabilities + Equity will equal Total Assets.

Why this works (one short sentence)
- An inventory misstatement in a prior year affects that year’s profit and therefore retained earnings; the balance sheet only balances when retained earnings include the correction (or the prior-year inventory is restated).

Quick rule to remember
- If prior-year ending inventory was understated → current-year opening retained earnings must be increased by that understatement.
- If prior-year ending inventory was overstated → current-year opening retained earnings must be decreased by that overstatement.
