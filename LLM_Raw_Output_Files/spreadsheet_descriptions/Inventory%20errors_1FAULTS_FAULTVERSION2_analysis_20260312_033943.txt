Summary — overall layout
- Three sheets (Sheet1, Sheet2, Sheet3) demonstrating an inventory error example across three years (columns for 2001, 2002, 2003).
- Each sheet contains: an Income Statement section (Sales, Cost of Goods Sold broken into Beginning Inventory, Purchases, Ending Inventory → COGS, Gross Profit, Operating Expenses, Net Income) followed by a Balance Sheet section (Assets: Cash, Inventory, Building, Land → Total Assets; Liabilities: Accounts Payable, Mortgage → Total Liabilities; Equity: Shareholders’ equity, Retained earnings → Total Equity; Total Liabilities & Equity).
- Years are shown across columns (2001 in column set B/C, 2002 in D/E, 2003 in F/G). Many values are hard-coded; many totals and links are formulas.

Key formulas and links (common patterns)
- Cost of goods available = Beginning inventory + Purchases (e.g., B10 = B8 + B9).
- COGS = Cost of goods available − Ending inventory (e.g., C12 = B10 − B11).
- Gross profit = Sales − COGS (e.g., C13 = C6 − C12).
- Net income = Gross profit − Operating expenses (e.g., C15 = C13 − C14).
- Balance sheet inventory cells reference the income statement ending inventory (e.g., B21 = B11).
- Retained earnings are linked to net income (e.g., B33 = C15) unless adjusted to absorb prior-year inventory errors.
- Totals use SUM (Total Assets = SUM(asset rows); Total liabilities and equity = SUM(liabilities + equity rows). Note: Sheet2 C24 incorrectly uses COUNT(B20:B23) (bug).

Per-sheet differences / notable items
- Sheet1
  - Baseline layout with given beginning inventories, purchases and ending inventories (B8=165,000; B11=179,800; D11=147,200; F11=203,970).
  - Income statement formulas link COGS, gross profit, net income.
  - Balance sheet Inventory rows pull directly from ending inventory.
  - Result: balance sheet does not balance (user prompt notes this); prior-year inventory misstatements are not closed out of retained earnings here.

- Sheet2
  - Explicitly shows inventory errors per example 7-4B: 2001 ending inventory = 179,800 + 17,000; 2002 ending inventory = 147,200 − 25,000; 2003 ending inventory unchanged.
  - Same linking of inventory to balance sheet.
  - Two issues: the balance sheet is still out by the error difference (as noted) and Total Assets for 2001 (C24) has a typo/formula error: =COUNT(B20:B23) instead of SUM — this will produce a wrong Total Assets and contribute to imbalance.

- Sheet3
  - Corrected presentation where prior-year inventory error has been closed into retained earnings so the balance sheet balances.
  - Ending inventories shown same as Sheet2, but retained earnings adjusted: D35 = E17 + 17,000 and F35 = G17 − 25,000 (i.e., prior-year error removed from current-period income and reflected in retained earnings).
  - All totals use SUM and the balance sheet balances (Total liabilities and equity equals Total assets).

Key values (examples)
- Sales: 2001 = 440,000; 2002 = 510,000; 2003 = 460,000.
- Purchases: 2001 = 220,000; 2002 = 180,200; 2003 = 167,200.
- Beginning inventory: 165,000 (2001).
- Ending inventories: baseline 179,800 (2001), 147,200 (2002), 203,970 (2003); adjusted in Sheets 2 & 3 as noted (+17,000 for 2001, −25,000 for 2002).
- Assets: Cash and fixed assets provided per year (e.g., Cash 100,000 / 169,070 / 170,540; Building 400,000 / 400,000 / 336,490; Land varies).
- Liabilities: Accounts Payable 225,000; Mortgage Payable 250,000.
- Shareholders’ equity given (e.g., 129,800 for 2001) and retained earnings tied to net income (with adjustments on Sheet3).

Overall diagnosis / purpose
- The workbook illustrates how inventory misstatements affect COGS, gross profit, net income and how those errors flow to the balance sheet via inventory and retained earnings.
- Sheet1 shows an unbalanced statement when prior-year errors are ignored; Sheet2 shows the raw misstatements (and contains a SUM→COUNT typo); Sheet3 shows the correct accounting treatment (prior-year error closed to retained earnings) so the balance sheet balances.
